To help in creating your map and working on it as it grows, you can use the following flow as a guideline:

1. Identify problem solving situations

Testing skills are used as part of your problem solving mechanism, so analyzing how you solve problems may be a good starting point. Here's a description of this process that Herbert A. Simon offers in "The Sciences of the Artificial":

Problem solving is often described as a search through a vast maze of possibilities, a maze that describes the environment. Successful problem solving involves searching the maze selectively and reducing it to manageable proportions.
How you perform this search, and in what ways it is effective, is what you want to pinpoint from analyizing problem solving situations.

Reflecting on how you solve problems is a different activity from actually doing the solving, and this retrospective analysis is a tool for discovering and analyzing your skills. In another book, "How we think", John Dewey presents a scaffolding for the reflection process, in the form of the following steps. To apply it for reflecting on your testing skills, think of a situation when you tried to solve a problem while doing some test activity,and identify:

  1. the difficulty you felt
  2. the location of the problem
  3. the possible solutions you found
  4. how you experimented with and tested solutions
  5. the conclusion regarding the acceptance or rejection of the suggested solution/s

Taking notes while thinking about each of the steps can help in the analysis of the situation

You can take notes right here...

2. Identify the skills you used

For each of the steps in the reflection process, think about what skills you used.

When identifying skills, it's essential to figure out what skills are, but also what other elements in the knowledge dimension are. Here are some heuristics which can help you to identify the skills and to not mix them with something else:

First, here is some info based on a table in the BBST Instructors' course differentiating between different elements in the knowledge dimension. You can find out more details about each of these elements by clicking on the comment button.

Facts

A “statement of fact” is a statement that can be unambiguously proved true or false.

Example

James Bach was born in 1623” is a statement of fact (but not true for the James Bach we know in the software testing community). A fact is the subject of a true statement of fact.

Concepts

A concept is a general idea. “Concepts are abstract in that they omit the differences of things in their extension, treating them as if they were identical.” (Wikipedia: Concept).

Practical terms

We treat the following kinds of things as “concepts” in this taxonomy:

  • definitions
  • descriptions of relationships between things
  • descriptions of contrasts between things
  • description of the idea underlying a practice, process, task, heuristic (whatever).
Procedures

Procedures are algorithms. They include a reproducible set of steps for achieving a goal.

Reporting a bug

Consider the task of reporting a bug. Imagine someone has broken this task down into subtasks (simplify the steps, look for more general conditions, write a short descriptive summary, etc.) and presented the tasks in a sequential order. This might be a procedure or a cognitive strategy.

This description is intended as a procedure if the author expects you to do all of the steps in exactly this order every time. The description is intended as a cognitive strategy if it is meant to provide a set of ideas to help you think through what you have to do for a given bug, with the understanding you may do different things in different orders each time. In such cases, the description is a useful reference point as you go.

Cognitive strategies

Cognitive strategies are guiding procedures students can use to help them complete less structured tasks, such as those in reading comprehension and writing.

Models

A model is a simplified representation created to make something easier to understand, manipulate or predict some aspects of the modeled object or system. It is an expression of something we may not completely understand in terms of something we think we do understand.

Differentiation between models and other elements

Deciding to use a state-machine representation of a program as a vehicle for generating tests is a cognitive strategy.

Slavishly following someone’s step-by-step catalog of best practices for generating a statemachine model of a program to derive scripted test cases for unskilled personnel to follow is a procedure.

This definition of a model is a concept.

The assertion “Harry Robinson publishes papers on software testing and models” is a statement of fact.

Skills

Skills are behaviors improved with practice.

Effective bug report writing is a skill, and includes several other skills. One of those sub-skills is the ability to take a visible failure and vary your test conditions until you find a simpler set of conditions yielding the same failure. This is skilled work. Getting better at skilled work takes time and practice.

Instruction about skills will often feature examples demonstrating skilled work, like “Here’s how I use this technique” or “Here’s how I found that bug.” The specific demonstrations might be of a procedure, cognitive strategy, or skilled performance. In many cases, it would be accurate and useful to class something as both a skill and a cognitive strategy.

Attitudes

An attitude is a persisting state that modifies an individual’s choices of action

Gagne, Briggs, & Wager, 1992, p. 48

Attitudes are often based on beliefs (a belief is a proposition held as true whether it has been verified true or not).

Metacognition

Awareness and understanding of one's own thought process

Oxford English dictionary

Metacognition refers to the executive process involved in such tasks as:

  • planning (such as choosing which procedure or cognitive strategy to adopt for a specific task);
  • estimating how long it will take (or at least, deciding to estimate and figuring out what skill/ procedure/staffing to apply to obtain that information);
  • monitoring how well you are applying the procedure or strategy; and
  • remembering a definition or realizing you don’t remember it and digging through web searches for an adequate substitute.

Secondly, try to identify a specific skill, one tied to the situation you are analyzing. Avoid writing down generic characteristics like "Communication"; you can communicate more or less effectively. What does "Communication" tell you? What can you get from it if you read it in 6 months from now? Will you be able to recall the exact skill and why you think it was a skill?

Another idea: think of skills as heuristics. Here are the four signatures of the heuristic from "Discussion of the method", a book by Billy Vaughn Koen about the engineering method for solving problems:

  • A heuristic does not guarantee a solution,
  • It may contradict other heuristics,
  • It reduces the search time for solving a problem, and
  • Its acceptance depends on the immediate context instead of an absolute standard
Do your skills match these characteristics?

3. List them under categories in your map

Categorizing your skills can help in some ways, because it adds structure. For example, after you have gathered data in your skills map, you can look for large differences between categories. Maybe one of them has many more skills than others. That may mean that you have an area of skills that you work on a lot, that you specialize in, and so you’ve discovered a main interest. Or noticing that you use skills from a single category often, can inform you that maybe it would be interesting looking at how you could apply other kinds of skills to solve some problems.

Don’t expect the categories to be perfect delimiters of skills. If a skill could work in two distinct categories, that’s fine, you can choose the one you think fits best. However, when creating categories, a good heuristic is that if any of the skills in your map could go into that category, then it’s too general.

4. Repeat

Remember, what's more important than the resulting map is the reflection process you practice with each time you go through the flow. With time and deliberate practice, the map will become more relevant and you will notice interesting patterns about your own skills evolution.

This process may become part of how you identify new skills you want to acquire, apart from discovering the skills you already use.

There are multiple types of reflection described in this article by Donald Clark. Here's one way of categorizing reflection:

  • Rapidly during an action.
  • Thoughtfully during an action.
  • Briefly as a review after action.
  • Systematically over a period of time after action.
  • Long-term as one attempts to develop formal or informal theory.
Which types are you performing when reflecting on your skills?

In case you need some inspiration for your reflection process, here are some ideas from the same article:

  • Seek alternatives.
  • View from various perspectives.
  • Seek the framework, theoretical basis, underlying rationale (of behaviors, methods, techniques, programs).
  • Compare and contrast.
  • Put into different/varied contexts.
  • Ask "what if. . . ?"
  • Consider consequences.

More ideas which can help in the recurring process of reflection over your skills:

  • If you identified some mistakes you want to eliminate, consider the queues that lead to those mistakes. What skills could you employ to avoid them?
  • Slow yourself down when you notice you are using some skills, to give yourself the chance to observe more.
  • Pick challenging tasks and notice your behavior while you tackle them.
  • Consider what sort of feedback you can get from listing the skills you used in a certain situation.
  • Identify what you would like to do differently in a similar situation, to be more effective in solving the problem.
  • Try to first notice without judging/observe without interpreting

A tricky aspect is to identify ways in which you might be fooling yourself. Here's a snippet from an article on LessWrong, by Aaron Swartz:

As Feynman wrote, "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself -- and you are the easiest person to fool." Our beliefs always seem correct to us -- after all, that's why they're our beliefs -- so we have to work extra-hard to try to prove them wrong. This means constantly looking for ways to test them against reality and to think of reasons our tests might be insufficient.

Finally, it's worth mentioning that this guideline is not meant to provide an exact recipe for extracting your skills and creating final list of skills to memorize. By repeating the process mindfully, you can customize it in a way that enables you to learn about your skills in a meaningful way, and to add more understanding.

Here are a few ideas, again from "The Sciences of the Artificial", about the difference between rote and meaningful learning:
Partly it is a matter of indexing: meaningful material is indexed in such a way that it can be accessed readily when it is relevant.
Partly it is a matter of redundancy: meaningful material is stored redundantly, so that if any fraction of it is forgotten, it can be reconstructedfrom the remainder.
Partly it is a matter of representation: meaningful material is stored in the form of procedures rather than "passive" data, or if stored as data, it is represented in such a way that general problem-solving processes and other procedures can readily make use of it.
