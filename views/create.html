To help in creating your map and working on it as it grows, you can use the following flow as a guideline:

1. Identify problem-solving situations

In the book "How we think", John Dewey presents a scaffolding for the reflection process, in the form of the following steps. To apply it for reflecting on your testing skills, think of a situation when you tried to solve a problem while doing some test activity,and identify:

  1. the difficulty you felt
  2. the location of the problem
  3. the possible solutions you found
  4. how you experimented with and tested solutions
  5. the conclusion regarding the acceptance or rejection of the suggested solution/s

2. Identify the skills you used

For each of the steps in the reflection process, think about what skills you used.

Try to identify a specific skill, one tied to the situation you are analyzing. Avoid writing down generic characteristics like "Communication"; you can communicate more or less effectively. What does "Communication" tell you? What can you get from it if you read it in 6 months from now? Will you be able to recall the exact skill and why you think it was a skill?

Think of skills as heuristics. Here are the four signatures of the heuristic from "Discussion of the method":

  • A heuristic does not guarantee a solution,
  • It may contradict other heuristics,
  • It reduces the search time for solving a problem, and
  • Its acceptance depends on the immediate context instead of an absolute standard

3. List them under categories in your map

Categorizing your skills can help in some ways, because it adds structure. For example, after you have gathered data in your skills map, you can look for large differences between categories. Maybe one of them has many more skills than others. That may mean that you have an area of skills that you work on a lot, that you specialize in, and so you’ve discovered a main interest. Or noticing that you use skills from a single category often, can inform you that maybe it would be interesting looking at how you could apply other kinds of skills to solve some problems.

Don’t expect the categories to be perfect delimiters of skills. If a skill could work in two distinct categories, that’s fine, you can choose the one you think fits best. However, when creating categories, a good heuristic is that if any of the skills in your map could go into that category, then it’s too general.

4. Repeat

Remember, what's more important than the resulting map is the reflection process you practice with each time you go through the flow. With time and deliberate practice, the map will become more relevant and you will notice interesting patterns about your own skills evolution.

5. Share the map in the collective repo

You can share your own map in an anonymous way by publishing it in the collective repo. At any point you can unpublish it, making it private.
